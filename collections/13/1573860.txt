Also in 1980, Silverstein bought the building at 120 Wall Street, which was constructed in 1930.[10] In 1991, Silverstein set aside 20 floors of 120 Wall Street to be leased by non-profit organizations, as an Association Center, with tax incentives for the tenants and bonds for Silverstein to undertake building renovations.[11][12] By 1994, Silverstein had signed up 14 nonprofit tenants for 120 Wall Street,[13] and the building was nearly at capacity by 1997, with 38 nonprofit tenants including the National Urban League and the Illuminating Engineering Society of North America.[14]
