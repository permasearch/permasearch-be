As in many estate settlements, Brodess's death increased the likelihood that Tubman would be sold and her family broken apart.[37] His widow, Eliza, began working to sell the family's slaves.[38] Tubman refused to wait for the Brodess family to decide her fate, despite her husband's efforts to dissuade her.[39] "[T]here was one of two things I had a right to," she explained later, "liberty or death; if I could not have one, I would have the other."[40]
