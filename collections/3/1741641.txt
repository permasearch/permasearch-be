In the 19th century, European scholars, archaeologists and missionaries visited often.[20] At the time it was an oasis in a poor state, similar to other regions in the plains and deserts.[66] Edward Robinson (1838) reported 50 families, which were about 200 people,[67] Titus Tobler (1854) reported some 30 poor huts, whose residents paid a total of 3611 Kuru≈ü in tax.[68] Abraham Samuel Hershberg also reported some 30 poor huts and 300 residents.[69] At that time, Jericho was the residence of the region's Turkish governor. The main water sources for the village were a spring called Ein al-Sultan in Arabic and Elisha Spring in Hebrew, and springs in Wadi Qelt.[66]
