The FCC also received letters from the public that advised against taking reprisals.[47] Singer Eddie Cantor urged the commission not to overreact, as "censorship would retard radio immeasurably."[48] The FCC not only chose not to punish Welles or CBS, it also barred complaints about "The War of the Worlds" from being brought up during license renewals. "Janet Jackson's 2004 'wardrobe malfunction' remains far more significant in the history of broadcast regulation than Orson Welles' trickery," wrote media historians Jefferson Pooley and Michael Socolow.[3]
