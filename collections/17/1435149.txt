By this time, the strike had severely affected the nation. A half million workers were laid off as companies lacked steel to keep plants running.[153] The number of railroad cars loaded in the week ending July 7, 1952, was the lowest since records had been kept, and many railroads began to suffer financial difficulty.[154] California growers faced a loss of $200 million because there was not enough tin to make tin cans for their crops.[155] On July 22, the United States Army shut down its largest shell-making plant due to a lack of steel.[156]
