Three years after Quiet's publication, David DiSalvo wrote in Forbes that a "groundswell support for the book’s thesis" resulted in "a sort of cultish affinity for introversion," but cautioned that "as with all movement-making arguments," they’ve "fueled ... shallow conclusions" that were not made in the book itself.[58] DiSalvo cited studies indicating that introverts' elevated sensitivity to personality traits can manifest as bias against extroverts in performance appraisals and reward giving.[58]
