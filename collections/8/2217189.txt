Carter began his first year out of office with a pledge to not critique the new Reagan administration,[255] speaking out after the assassination attempt on Reagan,[256] and voicing his agreement with Reagan on building neutron arms in the wake of the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan,[257] and later disagreement with Reagan's handing of the Middle East.[258] The following year, Carter called for bipartisanship to fix American economic issues,[259] and criticized the Reagan administration's handling of the Sabra and Shatila massacre.[260] Carter responded favorably to Reagan choosing to remain within the Camp David agreement,[261] with distaste toward what he felt was Reagan blaming his tenure for continued difficulties in policy.[262][263] In 1983, Carter judged the Reagan campaign with having falsified simplicity in solving issues,[264] and criticized Reagan for a lack of attention to human rights violations.[265] In 1984, Carter stated he had been wrongly presented as weak by Reagan due to a commitment to human rights during the previous presidential election,[266] and condemned Reagan for not making rescue efforts to retrieve four American businessmen from West Beirut.[267] In 1985, Carter rebuked Reagan over his handling of peace within the Middle East,[268] his support of the Strategic Defense Initiative,[269] and Reagan's claim of an international conspiracy on terrorism.[270] Carter's insistence that Reagan was not preserving peace in the Middle East continued in 1987,[271] Carter during the year also criticizing Reagan for adhering to terrorist demands,[272] nomination of Robert Bork for the Supreme Court,[273] and handling of the Persian Gulf.[274]
