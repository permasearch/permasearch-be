Pakistani President Pervez Musharraf - then as Chief of Army Staff - was responsible for sending thousands of Pakistanis to fight alongside the Taliban and Bin Laden against the forces of Massoud.[122][123][136][137] In total there were believed to be 28,000 Pakistani nationals fighting inside Afghanistan.[123] 20,000 were regular Pakistani soldiers either from the Frontier Corps or army and an estimated 8,000 were militants recruited in madrassas filling regular Taliban ranks.[135] The estimated 25,000 Taliban regular force thus comprised more than 8,000 Pakistani nationals.[135] A 1998 document by the U.S. State Department confirms that "20-40 percent of [regular] Taliban soldiers are Pakistani."[122] The document further states that the parents of those Pakistani nationals "know nothing regarding their child's military involvement with the Taliban until their bodies are brought back to Pakistan."[122] A further 3,000 fighter of the regular Taliban army were Arab and Central Asian militants.[135] From 1996 to 2001 the Al Qaeda of Osama Bin Laden and Ayman al-Zawahiri became a state within the Taliban state.[138] Bin Laden sent Arab recruits to join the fight against the United Front.[138][139] Of roughly 45,000 Pakistani, Taliban and Al Qaeda soldiers fighting against the forces of Massoud only 14,000 were Afghan.[123][135]
