In the light of their near-simultaneous emergence into the literary spotlight, Hazlitt presents the two as a contrasted pair.[446] While Irving[447] created a considerable stir when he burst on the English scene,[448] and was at that time the more popular of the two, to Hazlitt that phenomenon was a consequence of the current rage for novelty. Irving's writings made for pleasant reading, Hazlitt allowed, yet he believed that Lamb, whose writing under the pen name of "Elia" is Hazlitt's chief focus here,[449] was more original and deserved greater attention.[450]
