Congress continued to debate the economic and social status of the free population, with land reform identified as critical to realizing black freedom.[109][110] A bill drafted in conference committee to provide limited land tenure for one year while authorizing military supervision of freedmen was rejected in the Senate by abolitionists who thought it did not do justice to the freedmen.[111] A six-person committee quickly wrote "an entirely new bill" which substantially increased its promise to the freedmen.[112]
